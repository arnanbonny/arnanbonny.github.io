Project Overview

An end-to-end exploratory data analysis on the Superstore retail dataset. To mirror real-world conditions, I deliberately corrupted a clean version of the data with seven realistic quality issues, then cleaned it back as if I'd never seen the damage. The analysis that followed identified discounting policy as the primary driver of structural losses and produced three category-specific recommendations.

Python pandas numpy matplotlib seaborn Jupyter

Data Quality & Cleaning

I injected seven categories of realistic data quality issues — missing values, inconsistent categoricals, duplicate rows, mixed date formats, multiple outlier types, hidden whitespace, and legacy ALL-CAPS values — then resolved each one with documented judgment calls (impute vs. flag vs. delete).

Six of the seven were resolvable with column-level rules. The seventh — contextually implausible combinations (e.g. a 90% discount with positive profit) — was not. Every individual field passed validation, but the combination was nonsense. Single-column rules can't catch this class of issue; it requires cross-column logic, and even then, some "anomalies" may be legitimate edge cases (promotional giveaways, employee purchases). Resolving them required domain reasoning, not code.

This is the kind of issue that doesn't appear in cleaning tutorials but consistently does in production data.

Key Findings

1. Profit collapses past a 30% discount

Median profit turns negative once discount exceeds 30%, with losses scaling sharply at higher tiers.

2. The 30% threshold varies sharply by category

Splitting the same view by category reveals that the headline threshold is misleading. Each category breaks at a different point — Furniture at 20%, Technology at 40%, Office Supplies not until 50%. A blanket discount policy applied across all three is structurally costing the business.

3. Two sub-categories drive nearly all the losses

Of 17 sub-categories, only two carry negative total profit: Tables (over $10K in losses, with ~2 in 3 orders sold at a loss) and Bookcases. Both sit inside Furniture and drag the entire category's reported performance down — while Chairs and Furnishings remain profitable. The framing matters: "Furniture is unprofitable" is misleading; the real problem is two specific product lines.

Plotted as discount vs. profit margin with bubble size representing loss frequency, Tables and Bookcases sit alone in the high-discount, negative-margin quadrant.

4. Losses are geographically concentrated

The Central region has a 65.2% loss-making order rate, paired with the highest average discount (29%) of any region. The pattern doesn't replicate elsewhere, suggesting a regional policy or incentive issue rather than a market-wide one.

Recommendations

1. Replace the blanket discount policy with category-specific caps

Cap discounts at 20% for Furniture, 40% for Technology, and 50% for Office Supplies, aligned with the actual break-even points. Anything above should require an explicit business justification (clearance, expiring stock).

2. Restructure or exit the Tables and Bookcases lines

Repricing, supplier renegotiation, and reduced discount intensity should be attempted first. If unprofitable after those, run a basket analysis to test whether these products pull profitable attached items before considering discontinuation.

3. Investigate Central region incentives

The discounting pattern is concentrated and consistent, which points to a policy or compensation structure rewarding volume over margin. A regional incentive review is the natural next step.

Approach & Skills Demonstrated

  • Data quality judgment — distinguishing legitimate outliers from data errors; choosing impute / flag / delete per case with documented reasoning
  • Cross-column reasoning — identifying issues that single-column validation rules cannot catch
  • Business framing — translating statistical patterns into category-specific, actionable recommendations
  • Avoiding aggregation traps — recognizing when an overall threshold (30%) hides important category-level variation
